My name is John Harden, and I'm a data scientist and a journalist based in Texas working for the Houston Chronicle. I am a Univeristy on Texas at Arlington journalism and public affairs graduate. I am also a Johns Hopkins University data science fellow, expecting completion in May 2018.


As a data journalist my job is to take heavy data sets, compute them and then turn them into stories that the average reader won't glaze over. I started working for the Chronicle in 2014. Before that I worked in Dallas-Fort Worth for the A.H. Belo owned newspaper The Denton Record-Chronicle.


I won two investigative awards -- one from the Texas Associated Press Managing Editors and the second from the Society of Professional Journalists. At the Houston Chronicle my work and interactives were recognized by the Texas Press Managing Editors and the National Press Foundation.

Identity Part 1: PROSPERITY TO POVERTY

A couple of blocks south of Larry Sawh's resale shop in Sunnyside sit rows of barren, boarded storefronts. From the driveway of his business, he can point and name a few of his former neighbors. Marshall's Tire Shop. South Side Store Food-n-Moe. David's Furniture. Screwed up Records. Money to Loan. Sawh, or Mr. Larry as locals call him, says these once-mainstays along with many others helped the community - once known as Baby River Oaks and Black Wall Street - flourish, keeping unemployment low and hopes high. "This is a close knit-community, and for many people here, starting a business was a good way to support each other," Sawh said. But that was more than 30 years ago, before the oil busts and the recessions.
